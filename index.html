001 / FAMD 3375 and 4359 / Real Client

The DollHouse

Full brand campaign for a Houston boutique carrying 65+ women-owned brands. Competitive analysis, SCAMPER strategy, 9-piece capsule collection, editorial photoshoot with 5 models, website mockup, and a 5-node Runway ML workflow for ongoing AI campaign content.

"A brand with strong physical identity and almost no digital presence. The goal was building a full campaign without a traditional photography budget."

Runway MLChatGPTCanva AIClaudeGoogle GeminiSCAMPER
Project Overview

Real boutique. Real brief. No photography budget.

The Doll House carries a loyal in-store community and over 65 women-owned brands. The challenge was helping a brand with a strong physical identity launch its first proprietary clothing label using AI-generated content in place of a traditional shoot. The workflow takes a locked editorial model image and a swappable garment input and generates a lookbook photo, ecommerce product shot, and social media content from a single pipeline run.

Collection Details

9-piece capsule collection priced from $70 to $325. Competitive analysis run against Emerson Rose (24.3K followers) and Thrifted Threads (169K followers). SCAMPER framework applied across collection development and campaign strategy.

Target User

Small boutique owners who need editorial-quality campaign content without an editorial-sized budget. The workflow is reusable and requires no photography background.

002 / FAMD 4359 / Cultural Editorial

TupacReimagined

A cultural fashion editorial translating Tupac Shakur's 1990s LA aesthetic into two modern looks, one for Spring and one for Fall. Visual mood boarding and SCAMPER were used to make deliberate design decisions rather than direct recreations.

Vogue Reference
Runway Gen-4ChatGPTGoogle GeminiClaudeSCAMPER
Leather vest over flannel. Baggy denim. New York, 1994.
Oversized pinstripe blazer. Blue bandana. The original references.
The Reference / Original Aesthetic

Look 01 / Club USA, NYC 1994

Layered leather vest over flannel. Baggy denim. Gold chain. The casual luxury of someone who never had to try.

Look 02 / I Like It Like That Premiere, NYC 1994

Oversized pinstripe blazer. Blue bandana. Dressed up and dressed down at the same time. That tension is the whole point.

Color Palette Extracted

Grey, blue, brown, denim. His palette was consistent across looks. Muted, layered, and intentional throughout.

SCAMPER Application

Substitute — Leather vest replaced with a blue paisley linen vest. More sustainable, spring-appropriate, and consistent with his original blue bandana signature.

Modify — Fit adjusted to be more tailored and considered rather than relaxed and oversized.

Adapt — Color palette updated for Spring 2026 trends while keeping the cultural reference intact.

This process pushed the work past recreation into actual design decision-making on each element.

Spring Look / ChatGPT render via Runway Gen-4 Fall Look / ChatGPT render via Runway Gen-4

Iteration Details

01

Visual mood boarding using Tupac's actual outfits from Vogue's fashion legacy feature. Pulled his color palette, layering logic, and the tension between dressed-up and casual pieces.

02

Applied SCAMPER directly to the leather vest. Substituted leather for blue paisley linen. Sustainable, season-appropriate, and culturally consistent with the blue bandana he wore.

03

Generated photorealistic renders using ChatGPT, then rebuilt the pipeline in Runway Gen-4. Google Gemini used to create a video transformation sequence showcasing both looks.

004 / FAMD 4359 / Brand Case Study

FUBUReimagined

Heritage to Future / 2026

Rooted in culture. Built for the future.

A full brand impact analysis and three-iteration design reimagination of FUBU. One of the most successful Black-owned businesses in American history studied through a social, environmental, and economic lens. Academic research translated into real design direction.

Brand Strategy Impact Analysis Design Iteration Cultural Research ChatGPT Runway ML SCAMPER
Case Study Overview

From a $100K mortgage to $6B in retail sales. And what happened after.

FUBU reshaped what was possible for Black entrepreneurs in fashion. At its peak, over $350 million in annual sales. The brand gave Black consumers something created by people who looked like them and came from the same communities. But overextension through excess inventory was the breaking point. By 2003, FUBU had pulled out of the U.S. market.

The environmental cost was also unaccounted for at scale. A single pair of jeans requires approximately 3,781 liters of water from cultivation to shelf. Denim dyeing accounts for roughly 20% of global water pollution from textile treatment. At $350M+ in annual sales, this was happening at a significant and unacknowledged scale.

FUBU works better when the branding becomes secondary. The direction gets interesting once silhouette, utility, fabric, and proportion take over.

Research sourced from Weekly Activity 4: Impact Analysis. Academic recommendations informed every design decision across the three iterations below.

Design Iterations

01

Modernized Heritage Tracksuit

Gray track set / Quarter zip and full zip

This version proved that FUBU works better when the branding becomes secondary.

What worked: Straight-leg silhouette improved proportions right away. Smaller chest branding read as more premium. The quarter-zip packable concept added functionality. That was the first moment the brand started feeling technical and fashion-aware rather than nostalgic.

What needed work: Side panel striping still read as retro tracksuit. The silhouette sat in mall sportswear territory. Fabric looked lightweight rather than luxurious.

Key learning: Once silhouette, utility, fabric, and proportion became the focus, the direction got interesting.

02

Fashion Week Baseball Jersey

Black tonal cropped jersey / 2026 silhouette

It stopped trying to revive Y2K and started using Y2K as inspiration. That distinction matters.

What worked: Tonal numbering looked expensive. Matte black-on-black felt elevated. Cropped boxy fit matched 2026 silhouettes. This version felt closest to Fear of God Athletics, Korean fashion week streetwear, and monochrome luxury sportswear.

What still needed work: Mesh football texture still leaned slightly costume. Mixed fabrics would have helped, a matte body with tonal satin panels and rubberized logos.

Strongest feature: The tonal treatment. That made it believable as modern fashion.

03

Technical Utility FUBU

Cropped utility jacket / Forever21 collab silhouette

This is where the concept stopped feeling like merch and started feeling like a legitimate fashion label evolution.

What worked: Silhouette was the biggest shift. Cropped body, oversized sleeves, rounded shape, utility pockets, adjustable hems. This silhouette is very 2026. Tiny tonal branding made the concept feel premium. The logo became integrated rather than decorative. Matte ripstop direction made the garment feel functional, elevated, and gender-neutral.

Biggest opportunity: A modular system. Detachable hood, removable sleeves, packable body, interior harness straps, reversible lining. That's the direction modern technical streetwear is moving.

Overall design evolution: Iteration 1 was modern vintage sportswear. Iteration 2 was luxury monochrome sportswear. Iteration 3 is technical fashion-week streetwear with heritage roots. That third lane is the strongest. The inspiration became FUBU's cultural DNA rather than FUBU's old product catalog.

Strategic Recommendations

Reclaim the legacy.
With greater awareness.

Update silhouettes with cleaner lines, minimalist typography, and repositioned logo placement to modernize while staying true to the roots. Align with culturally relevant artists the way LL Cool J connected the brand to a generation in the 1990s. Replace mass production with a limited release model. Smaller, intentional quantities stabilize finances and reduce the environmental footprint tied to large-scale cotton and denim manufacturing. The brands that shape the next era of streetwear will be the ones that actually learned from the past.
