The DollHouse

Full brand campaign for a real Houston boutique carrying 65+ women-owned brands. Competitive analysis, SCAMPER strategy, 9-piece capsule collection with line sheet, editorial photoshoot with 5 models, website mockup, and a 5-node Runway ML workflow for ongoing AI-generated campaign content.

"A brand with a strong physical identity but almost no digital presence — the challenge was building a campaign without a traditional photography budget."

Runway MLChatGPTCanva AIClaudeGoogle GeminiSCAMPER
Project Context

Real boutique. Real brief. No photography budget.

The Doll House is a Houston boutique with a loyal in-store community and 65+ women-owned brands. The challenge was helping a brand with strong physical identity launch their first proprietary clothing label using AI-generated content instead of a traditional photography budget. The workflow takes a locked editorial model image and a swappable garment input and generates a styled lookbook photo, ecommerce product shot, and social media content from one pipeline.

Capsule Collection

9-piece capsule collection ranging $70–$325. Competitive analysis against Emerson Rose (24.3K followers) and Thrifted Threads (169K followers). Full SCAMPER framework applied across collection development.

Target User

Small boutique owners who need Vogue-quality campaign content without the Vogue budget. The workflow is reusable, scalable, and requires no photography expertise.

002 — FAMD 4359 · Cultural Editorial

TupacReimagined

Cultural fashion editorial translating Tupac Shakur's 1990s LA aesthetic into two modern looks — one for Spring, one for Fall. Applied visual mood boarding and SCAMPER framework to make deliberate design decisions, not just recreations.

Vogue Reference
Runway Gen-4ChatGPTGoogle GeminiClaudeSCAMPER
Leather vest over flannel. Baggy denim. New York, 1994.
Oversized pinstripe blazer. Blue bandana. The original references.
The Reference — Original Aesthetic

Look 01 · Club USA, NYC 1994

Layered leather vest over flannel. Baggy denim. Gold chain. The casual luxury of someone who didn't need to try.

Look 02 · I Like It Like That Premiere, NYC 1994

Oversized pinstripe blazer. Blue bandana. Dressed up and dressed down at the same time — that tension is the whole point.

Color Palette Extracted

Grey · Blue · Brown · Denim. His palette was consistent across looks — muted, layered, intentional.

SCAMPER Application

Substitute — Leather vest → blue paisley linen vest. More sustainable, spring-appropriate, honors the original blue bandana signature.

Modify — Fit adjusted to be more tailored and intentional rather than relaxed and oversized.

Adapt — Color palette aligned with Spring 2026 trends while maintaining cultural reference.

The method pushed beyond recreation into deliberate design decisions about each element.

Iteration Details

01

Visual mood boarding using Tupac's actual outfits pulled from Vogue's fashion legacy feature. Identified color palette, layering logic, and the tension between dressed-up and casual pieces.

02

Applied SCAMPER framework directly to the leather vest. Substituted leather for blue paisley linen — sustainable, season-appropriate, culturally consistent with his blue bandana signature.

03

Generated photorealistic fashion renders using ChatGPT, then rebuilt the full pipeline in Runway Gen-4. Google Gemini used to create a video transformation sequence showcasing both modern looks.

004 — FAMD 4359 · Brand Case Study

FUBUReimagined

Heritage to Future · 2026

"Rooted in culture. Built for the future."

A full brand impact analysis and three-iteration design reimagination of FUBU — one of the most successful Black-owned businesses in American history. Academic research grounded in social, environmental, and economic impact, translated into actionable design direction.

Brand Strategy Impact Analysis Design Iteration Cultural Research ChatGPT Runway ML SCAMPER
Case Study Overview

From $100K mortgage to $6B in retail sales — and what happened next.

FUBU's story is about impact, both intended and unseen. It reshaped what was possible for Black entrepreneurs in fashion. At peak sales of over $350 million annually, FUBU was giving Black consumers a brand created by people who looked like them and came from the same communities. But overextension through excess inventory proved fatal — Daymond John acknowledged that overbuying around 2001 was the brand's biggest mistake. By 2003, FUBU had withdrawn from the U.S. market entirely.

The environmental cost was also unacknowledged at scale. A single pair of jeans requires approximately 3,781 liters of water from cultivation to shelf, and denim dyeing accounts for roughly 20% of global water pollution from textile treatment. At $350M+ in annual sales, these costs were occurring at a considerable and unacknowledged scale.

"FUBU works better when the branding becomes secondary. The direction became more interesting once silhouette, utility, fabric, and proportion became the focus."

FUBU Assignment Rec.png — Academic recommendations from Weekly Activity #4: Impact Analysis. The research informed every design decision in the three iterations below.

Design Iterations

01

Modernized Heritage Tracksuit

Gray track set · Quarter zip + Full zip

"This version proved: FUBU works better when the branding becomes secondary."

What worked: Straight-leg silhouette modernized proportions immediately. Smaller chest branding felt more premium. Quarter-zip packable concept added functionality — this was the first moment the brand started feeling technical and fashion-aware instead of nostalgic.

What needed work: Side panel striping still screamed retro tracksuit. Silhouette sat in "mall sportswear." Fabric looked lightweight instead of luxurious.

Key learning: Once silhouette, utility, fabric, and proportion became the focus — the direction got interesting.

02

Fashion Week Baseball Jersey

Black tonal cropped jersey · 2026 silhouette

"It stopped trying to revive Y2K and instead used Y2K as inspiration. That distinction matters a lot."

What worked: Tonal numbering looked expensive. Matte black-on-black palette felt elevated. Cropped boxy fit aligned with 2026 silhouettes. Reduced logos made it more wearable. This version felt closest to Fear of God Athletics, Korean fashion week streetwear, monochrome luxury sportswear.

What still needed work: Mesh football texture still felt slightly costume-like. Could benefit from mixed fabrics — matte body with tonal satin panels and rubberized logos. Needed more technical construction details.

Strongest feature: The tonal treatment. That's what made it believable as modern fashion.

03

Technical Utility FUBU

Cropped utility jacket · Forever21 collab silhouette

"This is where the concept stopped feeling like merch and started feeling like a legitimate fashion label evolution."

What worked: Silhouette was the biggest improvement — cropped body, oversized sleeves, rounded shape, utility pockets, adjustable hems. This silhouette is very 2026. Tiny tonal branding finally made the concept feel premium — the logo became integrated instead of decorative. Matte ripstop direction made the garment feel functional, elevated, and gender-neutral.

Biggest opportunity: A modular system — detachable hood, removable sleeves, packable body, interior harness straps, reversible lining. That's where modern technical streetwear is going.

Overall design evolution: Iteration 1 → Modern vintage sportswear. Iteration 2 → Luxury monochrome sportswear. Iteration 3 → Technical fashion-week streetwear with heritage roots. That third lane is the strongest by far. The inspiration is now FUBU's cultural DNA — not FUBU's old product catalog.

Strategic Recommendations

Reclaim the legacy.
With greater awareness.

Update silhouettes with cleaner lines, minimalist typography, and repositioned logo placement to modernize while honoring roots. Align with today's culturally relevant artists — as LL Cool J did in the 1990s — to reintroduce FUBU to a new generation authentically. Replace mass production with a limited release model. Smaller, intentional quantities stabilize finances while reducing the environmental footprint tied to large-scale cotton and denim manufacturing. The brands that define the next era of streetwear will be the ones that learn from the past.
